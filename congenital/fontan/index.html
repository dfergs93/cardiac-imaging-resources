    Fontan Circulation

    Physiology

    Goal: Eliminate the subpulmonary ventricle; direct systemic venous return directly to pulmonary arteries (passive flow).

    Types: - Atriopulmonary connection: RA to PA (older, rarely used now) - Total cavopulmonary connection (TCPC): IVC and SVC → PA (standard modern approach, often with fenestration)

    Staged approach: 1. Norwood procedure (if needed): Restructure aorta; RV-to-PA shunt 2. Glenn procedure (Stage 2): SVC to PA (hemi-Fontan) 3. Fontan procedure (Stage 3): IVC to PA, completing TCPC

    CMR Assessment

    Critical Parameters

    • Single (systemic) ventricle EF and EDVi: Primary prognostic marker
    • EF >40% is favorable

    • Progressive decline predicts worse outcomes

    • Fontan conduit flow: Total superior + inferior systemic venous return

    • Compare to pulmonary artery flow to assess collateral flow

    • Pulmonary artery flow distribution:

    • LPA flow
    • RPA flow

    • LPA:RPA ratio (aim for ≥0.4 for balanced distribution)

    • Pulmonary collateral flow: Calculated as (Total PA flow) − (Fontan conduit flow)

    • Normal: <20% of systemic cardiac output

    • Significant collaterals (>20%): Bypass pulmonary vascular bed, reduce PA flow

    • Systemic venous collaterals: Bypasses Fontan circuit

    • Porto-systemic collaterals (suggest elevated IVC pressure)
    • Scan to include abdomen/inguinal region

    AV Valve Function

    • Atrioventricular valve regurgitation: Common and progressive
    • Quantify regurgitant fraction by 4D flow
    • Severe regurgitation increases risk of failure

    Additional Assessments

    • Ventricular function and fibrosis: LGE helps identify early remodeling
    • Pulmonary vascular resistance markers: Elevated Fontan pressure correlates with elevated collateral flow
    • IVC flow pattern: Retrograde flow suggests elevated IVC pressure (poor prognosis marker)

    Fenestration

    Intentional R→L shunt: Small connection between Fontan conduit and RA (or fenestration in baffle). - Allows decompression of elevated venous pressure - Trade-off: Mild cyanosis (lower SaO₂, typically 92–95%) - CMR findings: Expect Qp:Qs <1.0; visible shunt on 4D flow

    Long-term Complications

    • Protein-losing enteropathy (PLE): Albumin loss from intestinal lymphatic obstruction
    • Risk factors: Low EF, elevated Fontan pressure, severe collaterals
    • Atrial arrhythmias: Intra-atrial reentrant tachycardia (IART) common
    • Thromboembolic risk: Consider anticoagulation if persistent atrial fibrillation
    • Progressive ventricular dysfunction: EF decline over years to decades
    • Hepatic cirrhosis: Chronic venous congestion → fibrosis

    Imaging Follow-up

    Serial CMR (typically yearly in young children, varies by stability) to track: - Ventricular volume and function - Growth of collateral vessels - Progression of valve disease - Signs of protein-losing enteropathy
