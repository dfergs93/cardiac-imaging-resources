    Congenitally Corrected Transposition of the Great Arteries (ccTGA)

    Overview

    Congenitally corrected transposition (also called L-transposition or L-TGA) is characterized by discordant atrioventricular and discordant ventriculoarterial connections. Despite the abnormal morphology, the physiology is "corrected" at rest because systemic venous return ultimately reaches the lungs, and pulmonary venous return reaches the systemic circulation—however, the systemic right ventricle is at increased risk for ventricular dysfunction over time.

    Key Features

    • Concordance paradox: Two levels of discordance ("double discordance") result in concordant physiology
    • L-loop ventricles: Left-sided morphologic left ventricle (subpulmonary), right-sided morphologic right ventricle (systemic)
    • Levocardia (normally positioned heart) with inverted chambering
    • Associated lesions very common: VSD, left AV valve regurgitation (systemic mitral valve), Ebstein-like tricuspid deformity, subaortic stenosis, conduction abnormalities

    Segmental Anatomy

    Situs and Orientation

    • Usually situs solitus but heterotaxy is common
    • Levocardia with normal cardiac position despite inverted chamber arrangement
    • May be associated with other situs abnormalities—evaluate carefully

    Venous Drainage

    • Systemic venous return (SVC + IVC) drains to morphologic left atrium (on right side)
    • Pulmonary venous return drains to morphologic right atrium (on left side)
    • Anomalous pulmonary venous return is frequently associated—check all four pulmonary veins

    Atrioventricular Connections (Discordant)

    • Morphologic right atrium → mitral valve → morphologic left ventricle (subpulmonary, in left position)
    • Morphologic left atrium → tricuspid valve → morphologic right ventricle (systemic, in right position)
    • Systemic mitral valve regurgitation is common (acts as systemic AV valve)
    • Tricuspid valve abnormalities: Ebstein-like malposition with apical displacement and dysplasia

    Ventricles

    • Morphologic left ventricle (subpulmonary):
    • Small, compact morphology
    • In left position (appears as "RV" on echocardiography)

    • Gives rise to pulmonary artery

    • Morphologic right ventricle (systemic):

    • Dilated, thin-walled, less compliant
    • In right position (appears as "LV" on echocardiography)
    • Supports systemic circulation

    • Progressive dilation and dysfunction common in adulthood

    • Ventricular septal defect: Present in ~70–80% of ccTGA cases

    • Usually perimembranous or subarterial
    • May partially restrict left-to-right blood flow

    Ventriculoarterial Connections (Discordant)

    • Morphologic left ventricle (subpulmonary) → aorta (posteriorly positioned, arises from left-sided ventricle)
    • Morphologic right ventricle (systemic) → pulmonary artery (anteriorly positioned, arises from right-sided ventricle)
    • Great arteries typically anterior–posterior relationship or side-by-side

    Coronary Arteries

    • Mirror-image coronary pattern is typical
    • Right coronary artery arises from left-facing sinus (anterior)
    • Left coronary system arises from right-facing sinus (posterior)
    • Coronary ostia may be higher or in abnormal positions
    • Critical for surgical planning (during potential future procedures)

    Aorta

    • Arises from morphologic left ventricle (in subpulmonary position but giving rise to aorta)
    • Typically posterior and to the left relative to pulmonary artery
    • Monitor for:
    • Aortic root dilation
    • Aortic regurgitation (related to VSD anatomy or endocarditis risk)

    Pulmonary Arteries

    • Branch pulmonary arteries usually patent and normally sized
    • May show reduced flow if severe subaortic stenosis (left ventricular outflow tract obstruction)

    Associated Lesions

    Lesion Frequency Clinical Significance
    VSD 70–80% Permits left-to-right shunt; position determines surgical approach
    Pulmonary stenosis 25–30% Variable; worsens systemic RV afterload
    Subaortic stenosis 10–20% LVOT obstruction; may require surgical relief
    Left AV valve regurgitation 40–50% Increases systemic RV volume load; progressive
    Tricuspid abnormality 10–30% Ebstein-like malposition; worsens TR
    Conduction abnormalities 50–80% Heart block common; may need pacing; CNS heterotaxy may have situs inversus totalis with right-sided IVC/SVC
    Heterotaxy/asplenia/polysplenia 5–10% Assess situs carefully

    CMR Imaging Pearls

    Morphologic Assessment

    1. Recognizing the inversion:
    2. Use systemic venous return to identify the morphologic left atrium (receives SVC + IVC)
    3. Morphologic ventricle identification: trabeculae, papillary muscles, valve attachments

    4. L-loop: Morphologic LV on left, morphologic RV on right (opposite of normal)

    5. Chamber size and function:

    6. Measure systemic RV volumes and ejection fraction (may be reduced)
    7. Estimate subpulmonary LV size (usually small)

    8. Quantify left (systemic mitral) and right AV valve regurgitation

    9. VSD localization:

    10. Careful cine imaging in multiple planes to define VSD type
    11. Position relative to great arteries (important for operative planning)

    Flow Imaging

    • 4D flow or 2D PC through great arteries to assess:
    • Systemic-to-pulmonary shunt (if VSD present)
    • Systemic AV valve (mitral) regurgitation fraction
    • Subaortic obstruction gradient
    • Branch pulmonary artery flow balance

    LGE/Fibrosis Assessment

    • Assess for myocardial replacement fibrosis in systemic RV (related to chronic volume/pressure overload)
    • Fibrosis correlates with ventricular dysfunction and arrhythmia risk

    Associated Anomalies

    • Pulmonary and systemic venous return anatomy
    • Coronary artery origins and course (may require dedicated coronary CMR protocol)
    • Situs and other congenital associations

    Clinical Staging and Natural History

    Uncorrected (Native) ccTGA

    • Presentation: Often cyanotic if VSD absent or restrictive; may be acyanotic if large VSD provides adequate mixing
    • Young adulthood: Systemic RV tolerates hemodynamic load reasonably well
    • Progressive dysfunction: 25–30-year-old patients often show declining RV function and increasing valve regurgitation
    • Complications: Arrhythmias, heart failure, endocarditis risk

    Post-Surgical (if prior intervention)

    • VSD closure if hemodynamically significant
    • Pulmonary stenosis relief or RV outflow tract procedures
    • Double switch operation (rare, very young patients): Restores LV as systemic ventricle
    • Atrial switch + arterial switch (historical, less common now)

    Associated Findings

    Cardiac

    • Conduction abnormalities: ~50% have heart block (related to abnormal AV nodal location)
    • Arrhythmias: Atrial flutter/fibrillation common in older patients; pre-excitation (WPW) possible
    • Endocarditis: Increased risk with VSD and valve abnormalities

    Extracardiac

    • Heterotaxy: Assess splenic position, sidedness of vena cava, lung airway anatomy
    • Cardiac malposition (dextrocardia possible but rare in ccTGA)
    • Associated syndromes: Down syndrome (Trisomy 21)

    Prognosis and Management

    Natural History

    • Infants and children often cyanotic; cyanosis may improve with growth or spontaneous closure of PDA
    • Adolescents and young adults generally stable if uncomplicated
    • Systemic RV dysfunction typically emerges in 3rd–4th decade
    • Survival to adulthood without intervention is possible but long-term outcome depends on degree of associated lesions

    Management Considerations

    1. Asymptomatic uncomplicated ccTGA with large VSD:
    2. May not require early intervention

    3. Serial follow-up for RV size and function

    4. Restrictive VSD or cyanosis:

    5. Balloon atrial septostomy (Rashkind) in infancy if inadequate mixing

    6. VSD closure if hemodynamically significant

    7. Progressive RV dysfunction, valve regurgitation:

    8. Afterload reduction therapy
    9. Consider double switch operation (controversial; best in young children)

    10. Heart transplantation if end-stage

    11. Endocarditis prophylaxis:

    12. Recommended for patients with VSD or valve abnormalities

    Key Teaching Points

    ✓ Two discordances = concordant physiology: Abnormal anatomy delivers blood appropriately but with systemic RV at risk
    ✓ Mirror-image coronaries: Right coronary from left sinus (anterior) — critical for surgery
    ✓ Systemic RV dysfunction: The limiting factor; emerges decades into life
    ✓ High association with VSD, valve regurgitation, subaortic stenosis, and conduction abnormalities
    ✓ Heterotaxy is frequent: Always assess situs and splenic position
